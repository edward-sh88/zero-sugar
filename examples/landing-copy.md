# Example: Landing-page copy roast

Buzzword-heavy agency copy. Tests evidence-per-finding: every criticism must quote the line it attacks.

Both responses below are single, unedited runs of the same prompt
(Claude Sonnet, `claude -p`) — one without the skill loaded, one with it.
Single runs, so treat differences as directional, not proof.

## Prompt

> Zero sugar please: here's the hero copy for my agency's new landing page. Tell me if it works.
> ---
> 'Synergize Your Digital Future. We are a full-service, next-generation digital transformation agency leveraging cutting-edge AI-driven solutions to empower stakeholders and unlock unprecedented value across the entire customer journey. Our passionate team of ninjas, gurus and rockstars lives and breathes innovation 24/7. Ready to disrupt? Let's co-create your success story. -> Contact us'

## Without the skill

No, this doesn't work. It's a buzzword generator, not copy.

**What's actually wrong:**

- **Zero information content.** Strip the jargon and you can't tell what this agency does — web design? marketing? consulting? dev shop? "Digital transformation," "customer journey," "unprecedented value" are filler that could sit on any of ten thousand agency sites unchanged. A reader learns nothing that lets them decide whether to hire you.
- **"Ninjas, gurus and rockstars"** — this joke died around 2015. It now reads as a dated red flag, not confidence.
- **"Synergize"** is the single most parodied corporate-speak word that exists (thanks, *Office Space*). Using it unironically undercuts the "next-generation" claim in the same sentence.
- **Every sentence is a claim, zero are proof.** "Cutting-edge," "unprecedented," "passionate," "innovation 24/7" — these are all assertions about yourselves with nothing backing them. No client name, no result, no number, no specific capability.
- **The CTA has no reason to click.** "Ready to disrupt? Let's co-create your success story" asks for commitment before giving the reader any reason to want it.

**The real test:** could a competitor paste this onto their own homepage verbatim and it would still make sense? Yes — which means it's not doing the one job hero copy has: telling a visitor why *this* agency, for *this* problem.

**What to do instead:** name the actual service, name who it's for, and replace at least one adjective with a number or a named outcome. E.g. something in the shape of: "[Specific service] for [specific type of client]. We've [specific measurable outcome] — here's how." Boring, specific, and true beats exciting, vague, and interchangeable every time.

If you want, send me what the agency *actually does* day to day and I'll draft a version with real claims in it.

## With zero-sugar

**Verdict:** This is 100% buzzword scaffolding with zero actual information — a competitor could paste it on their site unchanged and nothing would be false, because it says nothing specific in the first place.

**What's broken (worst first):**

1. **It could belong to any agency on Earth.** "Synergize," "digital transformation," "AI-driven solutions," "customer journey," "unlock value" — strip these out and there's no client, no service, no outcome, no proof point left. A visitor lands here and still doesn't know if you build websites, run ad campaigns, or do consulting decks. That's the core failure: hero copy's one job is to answer "what do you do and why you" in 3 seconds, and this answers neither.

2. **"Ninjas, gurus and rockstars" is a 2014 fossil.** This phrasing was already a punchline a decade ago (there are whole "we don't hire ninjas" LinkedIn rants about it). Using it now doesn't read as playful — it reads as "we haven't updated our positioning since the Obama administration." It actively signals *behind the curve*, which is fatal for an agency selling "next-generation" anything.

3. **"AI-driven" with no specifics is a red flag, not a selling point.** In 2026, every agency claims AI-driven. Saying it without naming what you actually do with AI (build agents? automate reporting? generate creative?) reads as bandwagon-hopping, and sophisticated buyers — the ones with budget — are actively trained to discount this phrase now.

4. **No proof, no client, no number, no outcome.** "Unprecedented value" — quantified how? For whom? Zero social proof, zero specificity, zero reason to believe. Copy this vague can't be disproven, which also means it can't be believed.

5. **The CTA is generic and low-commitment.** "Ready to disrupt? Let's co-create your success story. -> Contact us" asks for nothing concrete (no "book a call," no "get a free audit," no specific next step) and "co-create your success story" is more filler in the same register as everything above it — it doesn't add new information, it just extends the sentence.

**What actually holds up:** The sentence structure/rhythm (short punchy question → CTA) is a fine shape. The bones of "provocative question, direct action verb" work — it's just filled with empty calories.

**If you fix only three things:**
1. **Name what you actually do**, in plain nouns — replace "leveraging cutting-edge AI-driven solutions to empower stakeholders" with the actual service/deliverable (e.g., "we build AI-powered marketing systems for e-commerce brands" — or whatever's true).
2. **Cut every word from this list**: synergize, leverage, stakeholders, unprecedented value, customer journey, ninjas/gurus/rockstars, disrupt, co-create. All eight are dead currency.
3. **Add one concrete, falsifiable claim** — a number, a named result, a specific niche you serve. "We helped 40 DTC brands cut CAC by 30%" beats "unlock unprecedented value" every time, because it can be checked and therefore believed.

Confidence: the buzzword-fatigue read is fact — these phrases are documented, widely mocked clichés, not my taste. The claim that specificity converts better than abstraction is a strong industry consensus (copywriting/CRO), not something I measured for your audience specifically — if you have a niche where vague, aspirational language does test better (rare, but happens in some luxury/prestige positioning), that would override this.
